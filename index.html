Analyzing Literacy Rates Around the World¶

By: Merliss George

Motivation¶

In 1916, John Dewey, an American philosopher and educational reformer, said that "Education is not preparation for life; education is life itself". In 2021, the global literacy rate was approximately 0.85.

A 0.85 out of 1 seems pretty good, doesn't it? At first glance, yes, but there is more to the story. According to the World Population Review, in 2021, approximately 773 million people do not have basic literacy skills. Of those 773 million, over 250 million are children. While a 0.85 global literacy rate is great, 773 million people still not being able to read is something we, as a society need to work on.

Now, you may be wondering why, why is literacy important? Well, it is important for a variety of reasons. Higher literacy rates are associated with greater economic growth, lower unemployment rates, healthier populations, and less crime. Given all of these positive benefits associated with literacy, why is it not more heavily prioritized by society? Why isn't literacy used as often to "level the playing field" so to say? Knowing how to read is one of the best ways to create more equality. Learning how to read opens up more doors to a wide array of opportunities that may remain out of reach if one does not know how to read. This is why literacy, and more specifically globlal literacy, is so important. Being literate is enough to improve one's quality of life and help people not only survive but thrive as well.

In this tutorial, we will analyze data about the recorded literacy rates around the world between the years 2010 to 2018. This data was obtained from Kaggle (will elaborate more on this below). Analyzing data such as this could be helpful in potentially identifying regions/countries where primary school education or literacy programs are currently succeeding or, if it is not doing as well as was hoped, the data could assist in identifying potential solutions to remedy the issue.

Follow along with tutorial if you want to learn more about the Data Science Pipeline and want to attempt to make some new insights about global literacy!

Linear Regression Model

Knowing this information, let's try to fit a linear regression model to specific regions to see if we can make predictions about what the average literacy rates will be 10 years from now in 2032. We are particularly interested in the Central and Southern Asia region and the Oceania region because the two of those experienced the largest changes whether it was positive or negative. Since these are the two we are interested in, let's fit a linear regression model to these two regions.

First we need to wrangle the data again to get only the relevant data, which in this case is the average literacy rate per year for both the Central and Southern Asia region and the Oceania region.

Hypothesis Testing

Aside from communicating the insights you gained by analyzing your data, a very important and crucial step in the Data Science Pipeline is Hypothesis Testing. Hypothesis Testing is a method of statistical inference that can be used to determine whether the data can actually support a hypothesis or not.

For this tutorial, I will conduct hypothesis testing on the data used above that dealt with the average literacy rates from 2010 to 2018 for both the Central and Southern Asia region and the Oceania region. Since the literacy rates were obtained from two different regions, the data would not be paired.

I am going to check for normality within my two datasets. If the p-value > 0.05, the null hypothesis is accepted (the data is normally distributed), otherwise the null hypothesis is rejected (the data is not normally distributed).
